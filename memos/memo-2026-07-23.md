- US markets – tech and China ADRs softer: The Dow closed down 0.01%, S&P 500 fell 0.14% and Nasdaq lost 0.57%. Nvidia rose more than 2% and Super Micro Computer jumped nearly 20%, while Micron fell more than 1%; the Nasdaq Golden Dragon index dropped 1.80%, with NetEase down over 6%, Bilibili over 3% and Alibaba over 1%. - Cross-assets – oil and precious metals higher: WTI settled 2.54% higher at US$86.48/bbl as Hormuz tensions persisted; COMEX gold gained 1.44% to US$4,135/oz and silver rose 1.57% to US$60.04/oz. The DXY eased 0.05% to 101.125, while US two- and ten-year yields rose to 4.3042% and 4.6626%, respectively. [[Eastmoney](https://finance.eastmoney.com/a/202607223817730438.html)]

- Hormuz – Iran threatens wider energy disruption: Iran’s military said the strait remained closed except for vessels using designated routes and warned that a US strike on Iranian infrastructure would put regional oil, gas and power assets at risk. The escalation keeps the focus on PetroChina, CNOOC, Sinopec and airline fuel costs. [[Eastmoney](https://finance.eastmoney.com/a/202607223817730438.html)]

- Alphabet – capex raised again: Second-quarter revenue rose 24% to US$119.8bn and Google Cloud revenue increased 82% to US$24.8bn, but Alphabet lifted its 2026 capital-spending forecast to US$195bn–205bn from US$180bn–190bn. Shares fell about 4% after hours; continued server and data-centre spending remains relevant to Chinese optics, PCB, power-equipment and cooling suppliers. [[Eastmoney](https://finance.eastmoney.com/a/202607233817779591.html)]

- Tesla – revenue beat, margins and cash flow miss: Second-quarter revenue increased 26% to US$28.24bn, but adjusted EPS of US$0.33 missed the US$0.51 estimate and free cash flow was negative US$1.09bn. The shares fell as much as 5% after hours, providing a mixed read-through for BYD, Li Auto, XPeng, NIO and the battery chain. [[Eastmoney](https://finance.eastmoney.com/a/202607233817780625.html)]

- Global AI infrastructure – spending plans expand: OpenAI reportedly raised its projected computing expenditure through 2030 from about US$600bn to US$750bn and plans to spend more than US$30bn on new data centres. Microsoft also signed a multibillion-dollar data-centre agreement with Mistral, while Anthropic plans to buy up to two gigawatts of AMD’s latest chips from 2027. It also pledged support for Beijing Stock Exchange development.
